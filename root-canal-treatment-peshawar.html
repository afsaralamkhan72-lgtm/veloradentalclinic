What Is Root Canal Treatment?

Root canal treatment, also called endodontic therapy, removes infected or inflamed pulp, the soft tissue inside a tooth containing nerves and blood vessels, when decay or injury has reached it. Once the infected tissue is removed, the inside of the tooth is cleaned, shaped, and sealed, stopping the infection from spreading further and, in most cases, relieving pain almost immediately.

At Velora Dental and Aesthetic Clinic, root canal treatment is performed by Dr. Fatima Malak, who holds C-Endo certification, a specific qualification in endodontic therapy, in addition to her general dental training. This means the person assessing your tooth and performing the procedure has focused, certified expertise in exactly this treatment, not a general overview of it.

Signs You Might Need a Root Canal

The most common signal is persistent tooth pain, particularly pain that lingers after hot or cold exposure, or pain that wakes you up at night. Other signs include a tooth that has darkened in colour, swelling or tenderness in the gum near a specific tooth, or a small pimple-like bump on the gum that may come and go. Sometimes there are no symptoms at all, and the issue is only picked up on an X-ray during a routine check-up, which is one reason regular dental visits matter even without obvious pain.

When to See a Dentist Urgently

  • Sharp or throbbing tooth pain that isn't easing
  • Pain that intensifies when biting down
  • Visible swelling in the face or gum near a tooth
  • A tooth that has recently darkened without an obvious cause

If you notice any of these signs, it's worth being seen sooner rather than later. An infection inside a tooth doesn't resolve on its own, and antibiotics alone, without treating the source, typically only delay the problem rather than solve it. Regular check-ups also help catch these issues before pain even develops, since early-stage infection can sometimes be picked up on a routine X-ray.

What Happens During Root Canal Treatment

The procedure is carried out under local anaesthesia, so the area is fully numbed before anything begins, a point worth emphasising since fear of pain is the biggest reason patients delay this treatment, when in reality the procedure is designed to stop pain, not cause it. Dr. Fatima uses X-rays to map the root canal system precisely before treatment and again afterward to confirm the result. The infected pulp is removed, the canal is cleaned and shaped, and the tooth is sealed. Depending on the complexity of the case, this may be completed in a single visit or split across two.

After the root canal itself, the tooth is often more brittle than a natural, untreated tooth, so a crown is commonly recommended afterward to protect it from cracking under normal biting force, especially for back teeth that absorb the most pressure when chewing.

Is Root Canal Treatment Painful?

This is the most common concern patients raise, and it's a reasonable one given the procedure's reputation. In practice, modern root canal therapy performed under proper anaesthesia is not more uncomfortable than having a filling placed. Some mild soreness in the days following treatment is normal, as the area settles, and this is manageable with routine aftercare guidance rather than strong medication.

The discomfort patients associate with "a root canal" is usually actually the pain of the infection itself, before treatment, not the treatment. Delaying the procedure typically means living with worsening pain for longer, not avoiding it.

Pain relief after successful root canal treatment is usually immediate or very close to it, once the source of the infection has been removed. Any lingering soreness relates to the mechanical process of treatment itself, similar to how a gum feels tender after a filling, and this fades within a few days.

Root Canal vs Extraction

When a tooth is badly infected, patients sometimes assume extraction is the simpler option. In most cases, saving the natural tooth through root canal treatment is preferable: extraction leaves a gap that then needs addressing, whether through a dental implant, bridge, or denture, all of which involve more time and cost than treating and keeping the natural tooth in the first place. A natural tooth, once treated and protected with a crown if needed, also tends to function more predictably than any replacement. Dr. Fatima will always explain honestly if a tooth is not salvageable, but root canal treatment is offered wherever it's a realistic option to preserve the tooth.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is root canal treatment painful?

Modern root canal therapy is performed under local anaesthesia and is designed to relieve pain, not cause it. Dr. Fatima is C-Endo certified specifically in this procedure. Mild soreness for a few days afterward is normal and manageable.

How many visits does root canal treatment take?

Most cases are completed in one to two visits, each lasting roughly 45 to 90 minutes, depending on the complexity of the tooth's root canal system.

Will I need a crown after root canal treatment?

Often, yes, particularly for back teeth. A root-treated tooth can become more brittle over time, and a crown protects it from cracking under normal biting pressure.

What happens if I ignore a tooth that needs a root canal?

The infection can spread further, leading to increased pain, swelling, and in some cases loss of the tooth altogether. Addressing it early usually means a simpler, more comfortable treatment.

How soon can I eat after root canal treatment?

You can typically eat once the numbness from the anaesthesia has worn off, usually a few hours after treatment. It's sensible to avoid very hard or chewy foods on that side until any soreness settles.
